Ibn Luqman House

Ibn Luqman House, located in Mansoura, Dakahlia Governorate, Egypt, is a historic site famous for its role during the Seventh Crusade. It is where King Louis IX of France was imprisoned after his defeat in the Battle of Fariskur in 1250. The house is named after Judge Ibrahim Ibn Luqman, who was the city's judge at the time. Built in traditional Arabic architectural style, the house features two floors and a wooden ceiling with intricate designs. Today, it serves as a museum showcasing artifacts, paintings, and historical documents related to the Crusades and Egypt's victory. Ibn Luqman House stands as a symbol of national pride and a testament to the resilience of the Egyptian people.

Tell El-Ruba

Tell El-Ruba, historically known as "Mendes," is an archaeological site located in Dakahlia Governorate, Egypt. It was the capital of the 16th nome of Lower Egypt during the Pharaonic era and holds significant historical importance. The site was a center for the worship of the god Amun-Ra in the form of the sacred ram, known as "Ba-Neb-Djedet" or "The Ram, Lord of Mendes." Tell El-Ruba also served as the capital of Egypt during the 29th Dynasty, which played a crucial role in resisting Persian invasions. The area contains remnants of temples and tombs that reflect its rich history. Today, it stands as a testament to Egypt's ancient heritage and cultural legacy.

The Virgin Mary Church

The Virgin Mary Church in Mansoura, Egypt, is one of the most significant Coptic Orthodox churches in the city. It is known for its beautiful architectural design, featuring grand domes, intricate religious icons, and stunning stained-glass windows. The church serves as a major place of worship for the Coptic Christian community and hosts various religious celebrations and events throughout the year. It stands as a symbol of faith and cultural heritage in Mansoura.
